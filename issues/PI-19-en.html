→ All three covenants handle indigenous and disability separately, but never as intersection.

This is one of Taiwan's most overlooked vulnerable intersections.

2. International Evidence

CRPD §11 (Risk and Emergency) + §4.3 (Tribal/Community Consultation)

States Parties must consult disabled persons' representative organizations (DPOs) in policy-making,

including indigenous disabled DPOs specifically.

CRPD §25 Health + §26 Habilitation

Requires culturally appropriate service delivery.

Indigenous disabled persons face dual barriers: geographic accessibility + cultural appropriateness.

CEDAW GR-39 (Indigenous Women, 2022)

§54-58 dedicated to indigenous disabled women — UN governance's first independent treatment of this intersection.

International benchmarks

  • Australia NDIS: Aboriginal & Torres Strait Islander disability rate 24.3% (general population 17.7%); Closing the Gap 2020 lists this as priority indicator
  • Canada PALS (2017): Indigenous disability rate is 2× non-indigenous (40% vs 22%)
  • New Zealand Whaikaha Ministry (est. 2022): Māori disability + tangata whaikaha co-design service model

3. Adjacent Taiwan signals

  • CIP + MOHW Social and Family Affairs (2018): Estimated indigenous disability rate 8-10% (vs national 5%) — but no formal yearbook tracking
  • Remote/island healthcare gap: of 32 indigenous townships, 18 have no public rehabilitation department
  • Indigenous councilors and disabled councilors never overlap — political representation blind spot
